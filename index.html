Calibration

Amplitude


Sneaking suspicion that wherever I go, whatever I do, I’m killing millions of tiny organisms.


A pamphlet they give us in school says that hot water kills more germs than cold. I start washing my hands with cold water.


They haven't told me about the sinusoids. The little waves in the air, the vibrations that become sound. How the sun moves, that's how sound moves. Up and down in circular motion, multiplied out along the harmonics possibly infinitely, smaller and smaller. According to its law, complexity of sound is its density, on a spectrum from a monophonic vibrating glass to a white noise waterfall.


I assume they exist and my assumptions make them up.


“Unfortunately, a signal’s amplitude has no one canonical definition. Strictly speaking, all the samples in a digital audio signal are themselves amplitudes,” 1 they go along the grain both ways, with a wave-like nature and a grid-like structure. Not all sound, but recorded sound, represented sound. It must be broken up into parts so small they disappear.


So small, and out of reach. General amplitude offered up to you in its place, the ratio-unit of decibels, click it up or down in your pocket. How it feels wrong to interfere with its course, pushing the waves around, shrinking them, expanding them to suit your will.


It’s always bothered me some people’s insistence on keeping TV volume on a round number, rather than judging the loudness itself. But then I’m no better, because I’ll leave the volume at 39 just to make them squirm, the same movement that the amplitude performs for us.

That's lame.

The first time I saw Uncle Steve use a record player


He lifts the arm up and down, repeating certain spots a few times, typical range seems like 3 to 10. Relation of number to spot seems arbitrary. Spot seems arbitrary.


I think it would be weird, then, to switch to a tape player. You can't do that on a tape player, or a CD player, or an iPod. But he updated through all of those.


“Why did Uncle Steve stop drawing?”


“Well, he used to draw the things he wanted to look at. Very good drawings, too, with interesting perspective. Of street signs and school buses. Then I got him a digital camera and he took pictures of those things instead, and he was never interested in drawing again."

Ghostwriters


“I’m not a biter I’m a writer for myself and others

I say a BIG verse I’m only biggin’ up my brother.”

—Jay-Z in “What More Can I Say”


Kedrick and I always end up talking about ghostwriters. Speaking in tidbits: “did you know Future and Detail wrote ‘Drunk in Love’?” “Did you know PARTYNEXTDOOR wrote ‘Work’?” “Did you know that Kanye actually sat down and wrote all of “Gorgeous” in the studio in Hawaii after DJ Premier said Jay Electronica was the best active rapper, to which Kanye replied, “No, I am,” cowriterless for a song to prove it?”


I told him my dad is also a ghostwriter, and he hates his job. I told my dad I’ve been making money writing papers for people on the internet and, to my surprise, he was concerned with the ethics of it.


Did you know Swae Lee wrote the hook for “FORMATION”?

Fatherly wisdom


I asked my dad why he stopped writing.


“Well, when I was younger I was very unhappy, so I wrote about unhappy people. When I got older, I got happier, and I didn’t know what to write about.”

Heads Up


Bluetooth audio is a lot like teleportation in Star Trek, streaming over to the speaker like Spock getting beamed up from the transporter. Audio transferred over Bluetooth is really not transferred at all, but reconstituted on the speakerside. The data is crushed into the smallest packet possible, thrown across the room, and then reassembled. So you’re not really listening to the same audio, and Kirk and Spock technically die-reform each time they teleport.

Depaul Bar Outside Seating


I can tell Declan is tipsy because his tongue gets loose. I think to myself, “it was a mistake to give him Taipei by Tao Lin.” Anxious and Catholic, Catholic and anxious, radical confession appears like catharsis, though, of course, it’s just a more sophisticated sort of lie. But I only recognize that because I’m susceptible to it too, just like everybody else. One of the things he says is that some of the best sex he’s ever had has been dead silent. Just like a second ago, when the business student from India who had a top five list for colognes ready to rattle off told me that a) he is a trust fund kid and b) he almost studied art, my thought is the same: “I know.”

Skill Level: Maxed Out


I like the games like Runescape where you get to level 99 and just chill. The ones where you train a skill. I think that's a nice goal in life, too. I'm not talking about perfection—it's not called level 100. It's just 99. In one skill or two? I'm being reasonable.


I fetishize them, especially their poetics, but video games have always been impenetrable to me. The maps are the most confusing part, then figuring out what you’re supposed to do, then controlling the guy, then parsing the useful information out of the all-encompassing sensory explosion.

Because of this, I spend a lot of time on first levels, tutorial levels. The calibration, interrupted by the invasion, at the beginning of Halo. The idyllic training island world of Kingdom Hearts. I'm sure it's supposed to take about 15 minutes but I spend weeks there, weeks before the transition to the stained glass circulars making up the floors of a black lodge-y underworld. How loud the footsteps are on that strange surface. And how the sound effect recoils after every five steps, creating a loop of steps, sounding the same even when you're moving in space. How the music stays at climax even when I’m lost and don’t know what to do to trigger the boss to come out. I take up the console when it is vacated by my mom and brother. I’ve seen my mom addicted to a few different games over the years; she unlocked every treasure chest in both Kingdom Hearts. That's one of those things you do when you just need something to do, but over here instead of over there. What I would now compare to the doomscroll. I remember her grind, getting the last straggling treasure chests hidden within the map. I accidentally reignited that addiction years later, showing her the phone game Clash of Clans, probably because we needed something to bond over. She kept playing long after I got bored of it. Honestly, I think was the last straw in my parent's marriage.


It makes me sad when I see people I love playing Clash of Clans.


I think this is also part of the reason why I always feel like crying on the train. Looking out into the sea of earbuds and YouTube videos and candies mid crush. I see: depleted energy bars.


My mom is definitely a level 99—in more categories than most. She's too smart. I love her so much and I hope all the time that she’s happy. She broke her arm so I'm going to Ohio to help her get her work done.

In my hand I have a toy


A blue plastic watch with a rectangular indentation instead of a face. I insist to myself that it is actually a small television, that if I stare right at the rectangle, and if I think hard enough, my imagination will appear on the screen. I can see it so bright. At tee-ball practice, I show the other kids the magic watch. A couple of the boys see it, too, so they gather around my screen. Other say I'm lying, that it's impossible for anything to come up on an unplugged piece of plastic. I have to be convincing. I keep watching and ignore the naysayers.


Then they start to see it, too.

The grid grew strong


like a newly transubstantiated picnic table,

asserting itself as the center then expanding along each axis

outward so that everything remained intact,

and all was now part of a big picnic.


Antithetical to the human heartbeat—the pulse, a constantly changing message, caught up in cycles of bodily response—is the rhythm of the grid: a perfect time, so perfect that, if there were to be a flaw—an overexcited electron reaches the finish line a few milliseconds early, it would be so miniscule it becomes imperceptible. Though it moves through time, it’s doing the opposite of moving.


The grid is an arranger,

arranges, takes what you give it.

acting as if placing pieces of tape into tracks, so that they may steadily slide along the playhead when called upon, while along the way nudging any outliers into conformity.


In Pro Tools, the grid defaults to 120BPM, 4/4 time, or one measure per second: the other human heartbeat.

Project Runway: Season 4; or, For Chris March


I love Christian Siriano, because my parents do too and I think that Chris March should have made the finals instead of Rami. My first grade teacher says she watches too and she is also rooting for Christian. I just realized that I still remember her full name. I look her up and see she is now a school psychologist in Palos Heights, Illinois. I think all the time about when Christian told the model to push her shoulders forward, not back, at the end of the runway for the turn, during the partner challenge with Chris March. Rest in Peace, Chris March. I didn’t think it was weird when you used you own hair for your collection's fur lining. I liked the lack of moderation too, how you used your hair in every piece, made it totally unavoidable. I thought it was very beautiful and very punk. I think it's important to be both. (I cut off four inches of my hair and I’m saving it so I can try to make a little music bow, more as an object than a tool, and my inspiration is Chris, how he literally put his body into it, and how people thought it was too strange, and how that made him sad, and how that made me sad, and how I didn't understand how someone so clever could get so little love from professional tastemakers). You were clearly the nicest, sweetest guy. I could see that the show was defeating you. I learned later that after those long days in the studio working on 2-day challenges come the cutaway-ed solo interview sessions, after Nina shades her eyes with a handful of score cards—her signature diss—gives you a shitty critique and they almost send you home, again. After all that you need more sleep than that.
